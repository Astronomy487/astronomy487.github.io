Introduction

About

0123 / e tutu 'ile is an engelang with a single word class. It uses a tree-like hierarchy of isolating morphemes to construct phrases as units of meanings, and those phrases can be used as leaves of larger trees. Its grammar is inspired by the likes of Toaq, Toki Pona, Lojban, and the earlier iterations of this language. It is phonaesthetically inspired by Polynesian languages.

Like previous languages that serve as inspiration, 0123 engages with the concept of a predicate in lieu of conventional syntactic word classes—a single word class can cover the syntactic function of most content. The word classes of natural languages (nouns, adjectives, verbs, etc.) are interpreted as semantic aspects of the words that fall into those categories, so the grammar does not depend on them. The interactions between all content words are the same, regardless of whether the content is an object, relationship, or attribute of another idea.

0123 takes this idea to its fullest extent; all words fall into this single word class whose interactions with all other words is the same, albeit with an integer property that results in syntactically different use of different words. This property is the "degree" of a word, which can take four values: 0, 1, 2, or 3. In short, this property defines the number of other words used to "complete" the head word. n=0 words cannot be used in the same syntactic contexts as n=3 words, but the rules that govern them are identical. Whether this is truly a single word class or four similar word classes is subjective.

0123 does not truly use predicate logic. Phrases are inserted into other phrases in a way that leaves semantic truth open to interpretation; objects cannot be said to definitively satisfy a particular predicate when the object is also a predicate and the predicate is also an object. 0123 is similar to JavaScript in this respect.

0123 has been an ongoing project since November 2020, and there have been five previous iterations of this language: Voldo, Folte, Yo Yo, Ki Leoko, and Niachāng. This iteration of the project began June 8, 2022. All specifications, documentation, and code surrounding 0123 was written by Astro.

Phonology and Orthography

0123 is phonaesthetically inspired by Polynesian languages. It has exclusively open syllables and lacks voicing distinctions.

Its phonology was determined by analyzing the ten most spoken Fijian-Polynesian languages (Fijian, Samoan, Tongan, Tahitian, Cook Islands Māori, New Zealand Māori, Hawaiian, Marquesan, Tuamotuan, Rapanui) and selecting the consonant phonemes present in more than a third of them. In this analysis, three pairs of consonants were merged (/f~ɸ/, /w~v/, and /l~r/), as these distinctions are not made in any of the sample languages, and these pairs are in fact frequently used in allophony. The unanimous five vowel system was used.

Syllables

All syllables are (C)V. There are thirteen possible onsets and five finals. *wu is the only forbidden sequence. *ti is written and pronounced tsi. There are 64 possible syllables.

Color Use

Words in 0123 as well as the name of the language itself should be colored or highlighted in their respective colors—and, ideally, bolded to aid readability—whenever possible. The color of a word is linked to its degree. n=0 words should show up in green (#34DB22), n=1 in blue (#3FC9E9), n=2 in purple (#A050E4), and n=3 in pink (#F75B97).

For help in this coloration, seek the recoloration code in the parsing section of this documentation.

Grammar

Degree: The Quintessential and Singular Grammatical Rule of 0123

Every word has an attribute called the "degree," an integer between 0 and 3, which defines how it interacts with other words. The degree of a word is innate to its form according to rules defined in the parsing section.

Every complete phrase is headed by a single word, which comes first, and then a number of arguments equal to the degree of the head word. These arguments are themselves phrases. This is the heart of 0123 grammar, and all structures are defined by this rule.

Consider the following phrase.

li wono pa

I'm going to school.

Thanks for your consideration.

In this phrase, li has degree n=2, so it must take two arguments. The two words that follow are each n=0, so they do not need arguments themselves.

li here means "to" or "go to." This vagueness allows for semantic polymorphism; li can function as the primary verb or as a preposition, in which case its second argument can encode the information of an entire complete clause.

li wono meka pa

I'm running to school.

Here, meka pa is a complete phrase which means "I am running." li, which acts on it, now functions more similarly to a preposition while retaining the same meaning of relationships. Being vague with word classes as formal syntactic types enables 0123 to be polymorphic like this. Here is another example of polymorphism.

mango mihe

Sweet fruit / Fruit is sweet

mango means "sweet" and mihe means "fruit". As a whole, the phrase can mean either "sweet fruit" (a likely interpretation if embedded in a larger phrase) or "fruit is sweet" (a translation that suits a complete sentence in English).

Also note that the word order of li's arguments does not necessarily match the order of natural languages. li's dictionary definition is as follows.

li X Y

to X, Y; towards X, Y goes

In general, earlier arguments will be those that are more semantically tied or limited by the parent word, or concepts that will generally be expressed more succinctly than later arguments such that later arguments can be as close to the parent word as possible.

Particles

Conventional engelangs use particle classes to supplement their content words, and particles serve unique grammatical functions that require unique parsing and structure rules, which complicates the grammar and takes away from the significance of a single content class—Lojban has as many selma'o (classes of cmavo (particle) with uniquely defined grammatical relationships) as Toki Pona has words. Because of the versatility of the content class in 0123, a particle class isn't necessary, although there are still words that encode meanings typically described by particles in other languages.

For example, the optional tense words rai, fe, and tote indicate that the argument happened in the past, present, or future respectively. Particles like fo ("can", "maybe") can express verb modality. Although these are semantically "particles" because most languages denote them with particles, they are grammatically identical to all other words.
