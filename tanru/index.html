Lojban tanru are terrifyingly logically ambiguous

Lojban is a language engineered to communicate logically unambiguous statements in predicate logic. All meaning is expressed through predicates. Predicates can be combined in a few ways; the simplest combination is a tanru, made by simple juxtaposition. tanru have no consistent method for translation into predicate logic, unlike other forms of combined predicates. This is contrary to Lojban's philosophy, and it could have been avoided.

How do predicates work

Lojban is a conlang that is designed to express unambiguous statements in language. It follows the definition of predicates/predications, used in math to express statements.

In predicate logic (and thus in Lojban), semantic meaning is carried by predicates (an abstract word class that includes the semantic roles of nouns, verbs, and adjectives all together). For example, the predicate "mlatu" (gloss "cat") describes a state, action, or relationship among a set of arguments.

Lojban$x_1$ mlatu
English$x_1$ is a cat
Logic$\text{mlatu}(x_1)$
$x_1\in\text{mlatu}$

Unary predicates are interchangable with the idea of sets.

You can use this predicate as a characteristic to describe a new argument. To use cat 'as a noun' instead of 'as a verb', you use the particle "lo" and then a predicate.

Lojbanlo mlatu
Englishsomething $x_1$ where $x_1$ is a cat
something that is a cat
a cat
Logic$x_1$ such that $\text{mlatu}(x_1)$
$x_1$ such that $x_1\in\text{mlatu}$

"lo [predicate]" translates to "something which is described by this predicate".

This consistent correspondence between predicates in Lojban and predicates in the logical sense makes Lojban really fun to use.

Predicates can also have more than one argmuent. The word for "social" has room for two arguments:

Lojban$x_1$ jikca $x_2$
English$x_1$ is social with $x_2$
Logic$\text{jikca}(x_1, x_2)$

The word for "someone social" would thus be "lo jikca"; something that satisfies the first argument of "jikca".

Combining predicates logically

When you use an adjective before a noun, like "beautiful cat", you are asserting that something is a cat, and also is beautiful. These corresponds to the Lojban predicates "melbi" and "mlatu".

To turn a predicate 'into a noun', you throw "lo" before it. But how can you turn these two predicates into a noun together? It would be awfully convenient if there were a predicate that meant "$x_1$ is both beautiful and a cat".

You could use a relative clause structure:

Lojbanlo mlatu poi melbi
Englisha cat that is beautiful

You could use "je" to create a composite predicate that literally means "$x_1$ is beautiful and $x_1$ is a cat":

Lojbanlo melbi je mlatu
Englishsomething that is-beautiful and is-a-cat
Logic$x_1$ such that $\blue{melbi}(x_1)\land\green{mlatu}(x_1)$

Both of these techniques correctly relay the information in a way that is unambiguously translatable into predicate logic. That is why I find the implementation of tanru comically bad.

tanru [Combining predicates illogically]

tanru are a third technique for combining predicates. It involves literally juxtaposing the two predicates.

Lojban$x_1$ melbi mlatu
English$x_1$ is a "melbi" type-of "mlatu"
Logic$(\blue{melbi}\odot\green{mlatu})(x_1)$

There is no good way to express this in predicate logic in terms of the components $\blue{melbi}$ and $\green{mlatu}$, like we could with the "poi" and "je" techniques. I use $\odot$ to represent this tanru composition, but this is logically meaningless. There is not a consistent way to infer the meaning of a tanru. .i lo jbobau cu na logji

In general, the meaning of a tanru comes primarily from its second component (for the place structure). "mlatu" secretly has a second argument (gotcha!), so "melbi mlatu" matches that:

Lojban$x_1$ mlatu $x_2$$x_1$ melbi mlatu $x_2$
English$x_1$ is a cat of breed $x_2$$x_1$ is a beautiful cat of breed $x_2$
Logic$\green{mlatu}(x_1,x_2)$$(\blue{melbi}\odot\green{mlatu})(x_1,x_2)$

In this case, the interpretation for $\blue{P}\odot\green{Q}$ is:

$$(\blue{P}\odot\green{Q})(x_1,x_2)\qquad \equiv \qquad \green{Q}(x_1,x_2)\land \blue{P}(x_1)$$

The structure comes from $\green{Q}$, and the role of $\blue{P}$ is to also mention that $\blue{P}(x_1)$.

The tanru for "computer user" comes from "skami" (computer) and "pilno" (user). The structure of the tanru comes from "pilno":

Lojban$x_1$ pilno $x_2$ $x_3$$x_1$ skami pilno $x_2$ $x_3$
English$x_1$ uses $x_2$ for $x_3$$x_1$ uses computer $x_2$ for $x_3$
Logic$\green{pilno}(x_1,x_2)$$(\blue{skami}\odot\green{pilno})(x_1,x_2,x_3)$

Now, the meaning of $\blue{P}\odot\green{Q}$ is different!

$$(\blue{P}\odot\green{Q})(x_1,x_2,x_3)\qquad \equiv \qquad \green{Q}(x_1,x_2,x_3)\land \blue{P}(x_2)$$

Now $\blue{P}$ is adding qualifying information about $x_2$, not $x_1$!

The "je" and "poi" techniques allow you to consistently describe what you mean by composing $\blue{P}$ and $\green{Q}$. The tanru $\odot$ composition is inconsistent. The reference grammar itself addresses this:

The meaning of a tanru is usually at least partly ambiguous: skami pilno could refer to a computer that is a user, or to a user of computers. There are a variety of ways that the modifier component can be related to the modified component. … Making tanru is essentially a poetic or creative act, not a science.

This ambiguity is hilariously incompatible with the goals of Lojban. This implementation of tanru is nonsensical and annoying and silly. But because this meaning comes from juxtaposition (no "poi" or "je" needed), this is the most common kind of composite selbri! It is all over the place!

On lujvo

Of the three techniques ("poi", "je", and tanru) for making composite predicates, the latter two enable the use of lujvo. lujvo are phonoaesthetically very cool.

Root predicates have shortened forms, and when a predicate composition is being made, the components can use their shortened forms instead of longer forms. They are semantically identical.

For the "je" technique, this looks like:

Lojban$x_1$ barda je bloti$x_1$ barjevlo'i
English$x_1$ is-big and is-a-boat$x_1$ is a big boat
Logic$\blue{barda}(x_1)\land\green{bloti}(x_1)$$\blue{barda}(x_1)\land\green{bloti}(x_1)$

For tanru, this looks like:

Lojban$x_1$ barda bloti$x_1$ barlo'i
English$x_1$ is a big kind-of boat$x_1$ is a big kind-of boat
Logic$(\blue{barda}\odot\green{bloti})(x_1)$$(\blue{barda}\odot\green{bloti})(x_1)$

tanru interpretations

Previously, I showed off how the tanru of P and Q (symbolically $\blue{P}\odot\green{Q}$) has inconsistent interpretations. The essence of the tanru lies in Q, which takes all the arguments, whereas P adds some extra information about one of the arguments.

  1. P elaborates on $x_1$; $(\blue{P}\odot\green{Q})(x_1,\cdots)\equiv\green{Q}(x_1,\cdots)\land \blue{P}(x_1)$
    • barda bloti; "big boat"
    • melbi mlatu; "beautiful cat"
  2. P elaborates on $x_2$; $(\blue{P}\odot\green{Q})(x_1,\cdots)\equiv\green{Q}(x_1,\cdots)\land \blue{P}(x_2)$
    • skami pilno; "computer user"

In the first kind, it's easy enough to turn this into an expression using "je"; "$x_1$ P Q" is the same as "$x_1$ P je Q".

In the second kind, it's more difficult to get P to elaborate on $x_2$ in a "je" expresion. But I guess it's possible: "$x_1$ skami pilno $x_2$" would be the same as "$x_1$ se ke skami je se pilno ke'e $x_1$". What a mess. It uses the passive voice twice, nested within itself.

If anything, this points to the fact that arguments should've been better ordered. pilno could have been defined as either of these:

order 1$x_1$ pilno $x_2$user $x_1$ uses tool $x_2$
order 2$x_1$ pilno2 $x_2$tool $x_1$ is used by user $x_2$

But Lojban chose the first one because it matches the user-is-subject tool-is-object order we expect from English. But those are just semantic differences! Lojban didn't have to do it!

If Lojban used that second order, pilno2, then "$x_1$ skami pilno2 $x_2$" ($x_1$ is a computer used by $x_2$) could simply be "$x_1$ skami je pilno". The fact is that tanru more often want to qualify the tool in the pilno relation ($x_2$ for pilno, but conveniently $x_1$ for pilno2).

If we're dreaming here, if every predicate had more carefully ordered its arguments, tanru could have been made logically consistent by always having P elaborate on $x_1$.

I don't even want to get into the mess of tanru-of-tanru, which are non-associative and even less consistently interpreted.

$$\blue{P}\odot\green{Q}\odot\pink{R}=(\blue{P}\odot\green{Q})\odot\pink{R}\neq\blue{P}\odot(\green{Q}\odot\pink{R})$$

tanru don't allow metaphor

This is tangentially related.

The combination of "jikca" (social) and "toldi" (butterfly), using the unambiguous "je", would be:

Lojban$x_1$ jikca toldi
English$x_1$ is-social and is-a-butterfly
$x_1$ is a social butterfly
Logic$\blue{jikca}(x_1)\land\green{toldi}(x_1)$

The English phrase "social butterfly", however, is an idiom! If a Lojban user said in a real sentence "lo mi pendo cu jikca toldi", "my friend is a social butterfly", they would probably be using a metaphor to describe the personality of their friend.

According to the reference grammar, this is literally wrong.

You aren't supposed to use metaphors in Lojban. This is tantamount to lying: your friend is not a butterfly.

In general, however, the meaning of a tanru is determined by the literal meaning of its components, and not by any connotations or figurative meanings. Thus "sutra tavla" ("fast talker") would not necessarily imply any trickery or deception, unlike the English idiom, and a "jikca toldi" ("social butterfly") must always be an insect with large brightly-colored wings, of the family Lepidoptera.

Had they said "lo mi pendo cu jikca je toldi", this imposition would make sense. The interpretation in predicate logic is strict.

Lojbanlo mi pendo cu jikca je toldi
Englishmy friend is social and is a butterfly
Logic$\blue{jikca}(x)\land\green{toldi}(x)$ where $x$ is my friend

But in a tanru, where $\blue{P}\odot\green{Q}$ is so ambiguously defined, what's so wrong about using $\blue{P}\odot\green{Q}$ to communicate a reasonable metaphor using the concept of a butterfly?

Lojbanlo mi pendo cu jikca toldi
Englishmy friend is a social butterfly
*absolutely cannot be metaphorical
*friend is literally a butterfly
Logic$(\blue{jikca}\odot\green{toldi})(x)$ where $x$ is my friend

What would happen if I used the tanru "jikca toldi" to communicate the English-originating metaphor of a social butterfly? What will happen?

Will it be grammatically incorrect? No, the syntax is all sound.

Would the listener not understand what I'm saying?

Would I be lying?
