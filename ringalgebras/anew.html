The complex numbers $\mathbb C$ are cool

The complex numbers $\mathbb C$ are cool! They come in the form $a+b i$, where $a,b\in\mathbb R$, thus they are a vector space with basis $\{1,i\}$. Addition can simply be executed component-wise. But this is no ordinary vector space, for it is also equipped with a product operation! It distributes over addition, thus

$$(a+b i)(c+d i)=(ac)+(ad+bc)i+(bd)i^2$$

Look at that last addentâ€”$i^2$ isn't in our basis, get out of here! The complex numbers are characterized by the fact that $i^2:=-1$, so the $i^2$ component can go join the real component:

$$(a+b i)(c+d i)=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i$$

Once you have both additions and products, you can really have any analytic function, since

$$f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^n}{n!}\frac{d^nf}{dx^n}(0)$$

only depends on computing powers of $x$, scaling them, and adding them together. This is how, only assuming that $i^2:=-1$, you can retrieve cool things like $e^{ix}=\cos x+i\sin x$. It's all just power series!

Other algebras in two dimensions

What if we defined $i^2$ to simplify to some other linear combination over the basis $\{1,i\}$? Let $i^2:=X+Yi$. Then we instead get the product rule

$$(a+b i)(c+d i)=(ac+Xbd)+(ad+bc+Ybd)i$$

This covers the behavior of any possible two-dimensional algebra, with the real component on basis element $1$ (which also happens to be the multiplicative identity, wow), and a second component on the basis element we're still calling $i$.

All the algebras

Let's broaden our scope! Let's say that our basis set is now $Z=\{z_0,z_1,z_2,\cdots\}$. I'm not even going to make any claims about one of these being a "real" basis element, or even about the multiplicative identity appearing as some linear combination over the basis. All I know is that a number in this algebra is represented as

$$a=a_0z_0+a_1b_1+a_2b_2+\cdots=\sum_na_nz_n$$

And when two such numbers multiply, it will distribute over addition, and we will have to consider many products:

$$ab=(a_0z_0+a_1z_1+\cdots)(b_0z_0+b_1z_1+\cdots)=\sum_{n,m}a_nb_mz_nz_m$$
